## Day 1-2: Prague (3/16-17)

### Reformation History

Prague is where the Reformation **began before Luther**. **Jan Hus (1369-1415)** was a Czech priest and rector of Charles University who challenged the Catholic Church's corruption — especially the sale of indulgences — a full century before Luther. He was burned at the stake as a heretic at the Council of Constance (1415). His followers, the **Hussites**, launched wars that shook Europe and laid the groundwork for Luther's movement. Luther himself acknowledged Hus as a predecessor.

### Key Sites

- **Bethlehem Chapel (Betlemska kaple)** — Where Hus preached in Czech (not Latin), revolutionary for the time
- **Old Town Square** — Jan Hus Memorial statue stands at the center
- **Charles University (Karolinum)** — Where Hus served as rector
- **Tyn Church** — Was a Hussite church; note the chalice symbol (Hussites insisted laity receive both bread AND wine)

### Practical Tips

- Prague uses **Czech Koruna (CZK)**, not Euro. Cards widely accepted but carry some cash for small shops
- Public transport: buy a 72-hour pass for trams/metro
- March weather: ~2-10°C, bring layers and a rain jacket
- Old Town is walkable; most reformation sites are close together

## Day 3: Wittenberg (3/18)

### Reformation History

The **birthplace of the Protestant Reformation**. On October 31, 1517, **Martin Luther** nailed his **95 Theses** to the door of the Castle Church, challenging the sale of indulgences. Wittenberg was where Luther lived, taught, and developed his theology of salvation by grace through faith alone (*sola gratia, sola fide*).

### Key Sites

- **Castle Church (Schlosskirche)** — The famous door where the 95 Theses were posted; Luther's tomb is inside
- **St. Mary's Church (Stadtkirche)** — Where Luther regularly preached; the "mother church of the Reformation"
- **Luther House (Lutherhaus)** — Luther's residence, now the world's largest Reformation museum
- **Melanchthon House** — Home of Philip Melanchthon, Luther's closest collaborator
- **Luther Oak** — Marks the spot where Luther burned the papal bull threatening his excommunication (1520)

### Practical Tips

- Small town — all sites within walking distance
- You'll have limited time since you're in transit; prioritize **Castle Church + Lutherhaus**
- Germany uses **Euro**

## Day 3-4: Leipzig (3/18-19)

### Reformation History

Leipzig hosted the **Leipzig Debate (1519)**, where Luther debated theologian Johann Eck. During this debate, Luther was pushed to admit he agreed with some of Jan Hus's positions — connecting Prague to Wittenberg historically. This debate radicalized Luther's break from Rome. Leipzig was also where **J.S. Bach** later served as cantor, continuing the rich Protestant musical tradition Luther started.

### Key Sites

- **St. Thomas Church (Thomaskirche)** — Bach's church; Luther preached here in 1539
- **St. Nicholas Church (Nikolaikirche)** — Important for both Reformation history and the 1989 Peaceful Revolution
- **Old Town Hall (Altes Rathaus)** — Site near the Leipzig Debate

### Practical Tips

- Larger city with good public transport (tram system)
- The city center is compact and walkable

---

## Day 4: Erfurt (3/19)

### Reformation History

Erfurt is where **Luther's spiritual journey began**. He studied at the University of Erfurt (1501-1505), and after a terrifying thunderstorm, vowed to become a monk. He entered the **Augustinian Monastery** here in 1505, where he underwent intense spiritual struggles (*Anfechtungen*) about sin, guilt, and God's righteousness — the very struggles that eventually led to his breakthrough understanding of justification by faith.

### Key Sites

- **Augustinian Monastery (Augustinerkloster)** — Where Luther lived as a monk; you can see his cell. This is perhaps the most spiritually moving Luther site
- **Erfurt Cathedral (Dom)** — Where Luther was ordained as a priest in 1507
- **St. Severus Church** — Adjacent to the cathedral on the cathedral hill
- **University of Erfurt** — Where Luther studied philosophy and law before his monastic turn

### Practical Tips

- The cathedral and monastery are the must-sees
- Beautiful medieval old town; Kramerbrucke (Merchants' Bridge) is worth a quick walk

---

## Day 5: Eisenach (3/20)

### Reformation History

After the **Diet of Worms** (1521), Luther was "kidnapped" by allies of Frederick the Wise and hidden at **Wartburg Castle** for nearly a year. During this time, disguised as "Junker Jorg" (Knight George), Luther translated the **New Testament from Greek into German** in just 11 weeks — a landmark achievement that standardized the German language and put Scripture into the hands of ordinary people.

### Key Sites

- **Wartburg Castle (UNESCO)** — Luther's room where he translated the Bible is preserved. The inkstain on the wall (where Luther allegedly threw an inkwell at the devil) is legendary, though the original stain was chipped away by souvenir hunters
- **Luther House Eisenach** — Where Luther stayed as a student (1498-1501)
- **Bach House** — Bach was born in Eisenach

### Practical Tips

- Wartburg Castle requires a **steep uphill walk (~20 min)** or shuttle — wear good shoes
- Allow 1.5-2 hours for the castle tour
- Interior rooms may only be accessible by guided tour

## Day 5 (evening): Frankfurt (3/20)

### Reformation History

Frankfurt was not a primary Reformation city, but it hosted important **Imperial Diets** and was a center of the **printing industry** that spread Reformation ideas. The Frankfurt Book Fair, already important by Luther's time, became a channel for distributing Protestant writings across Europe.

### Practical Tips

- Mostly a transit stop
- If you have evening free time, **Romerberg** (old town square) is worth a walk

---

## Day 6: Worms (3/21)

### Reformation History

One of the most dramatic moments in church history. At the **Diet of Worms (1521)**, Luther stood before Emperor Charles V and the assembled princes and refused to recant his writings. His famous declaration: **"Here I stand, I can do no other. God help me. Amen."** He was declared an outlaw, but this moment cemented the Reformation's survival.

### Key Sites

- **Luther Monument (Lutherdenkmal)** — The largest Reformation monument in the world, featuring Luther surrounded by key pre-reformers and reformers including Hus, Wycliffe, and others
- **Heylshof Garden** — Near the site of the Diet
- **Worms Cathedral (Dom St. Peter)** — The Romanesque cathedral that stood during Luther's trial
- **Trinity Church** — Protestant church built after the Reformation

### Practical Tips

- Small city; the main sites can be covered in 2-3 hours
- The Luther Monument alone is worth the stop

---

## Day 6-7: Zurich (3/21-22)

### Reformation History

Zurich was the center of the **Swiss Reformation** under **Huldrych Zwingli (1484-1531)**. Zwingli reformed Zurich independently of Luther, beginning in 1519. He emphasized Scripture's authority, removed images from churches, and clashed with Luther over the Lord's Supper (Zwingli saw it as symbolic; Luther insisted on Christ's real presence). Zwingli died in battle at Kappel in 1531. Zurich was also where the **Anabaptist movement** began (1525), led by Conrad Grebel and Felix Manz, who insisted on believer's baptism — they were persecuted by both Catholics AND other Protestants.

### Key Sites

- **Grossmunster** — Zwingli's church, where he preached and launched the Reformation in Zurich. The Reformation museum is in the cloister
- **Wasserkirche** — Where Felix Manz was drowned (executed) for his Anabaptist beliefs (1527)
- **Fraumunster** — Notable for Chagall's stained glass windows
- **Zwingli statue** next to Wasserkirche

### Practical Tips

- Switzerland uses **Swiss Franc (CHF)**, not Euro — significantly more expensive than Germany
- Zurich is very walkable along the river/lake
- Tap water is excellent and free

---

## Day 7: Lucerne (3/22)

### Reformation History

Lucerne remained **staunchly Catholic** during the Reformation and was a leading member of the Catholic cantons that opposed Zurich and the Protestant cantons. The religious divide in Switzerland led to the **Kappel Wars** — the conflict in which Zwingli was killed. Lucerne's **Jesuit Church** (built 1666-1677) was the first large Baroque church in Switzerland and symbolized the Counter-Reformation.

### Key Sites

- **Jesuit Church (Jesuitenkirche)** — Counter-Reformation symbol
- **Chapel Bridge (Kapellbrucke)** — Medieval bridge with paintings depicting the city's history
- **Church of St. Leodegar** — Catholic foundation

### Practical Tips

- Mostly a transit city — 1-2 hours is enough for a quick walk
- Beautiful lakeside setting

---

## Day 7-8: Geneva (3/22-23)

### Reformation History

Geneva became the **"Protestant Rome"** under **John Calvin (1509-1564)**. The French-born theologian arrived in 1536 and transformed Geneva into a model Reformed city. Calvin's theology — predestination, God's sovereignty, church discipline — profoundly influenced Presbyterianism, the Reformed tradition, Puritanism, and through them, the shaping of modern democracy. His **Institutes of the Christian Religion** is one of the most important works in Protestant theology. **Theodore Beza** succeeded Calvin and continued his work.

### Key Sites

- **St. Pierre Cathedral** — Calvin's church; you can see his chair. Climb the tower for city views
- **Reformation Wall (Mur des Reformateurs)** — Massive monument featuring Calvin, Beza, Knox, and Farel
- **Calvin Auditorium** — Where Calvin lectured
- **International Museum of the Reformation** — Excellent museum next to St. Pierre
- **Calvin's grave** — In the Plainpalais cemetery, marked only by a simple "J.C." (per his wishes for humility)

### Practical Tips

- Also uses **CHF** — expensive city
- The Old Town (Vieille Ville) with the cathedral is compact and walkable
- Reformation Wall is in Parc des Bastions, a pleasant walk from the old town

---

## Day 8-9: Milan & Florence (3/23-24)

### Reformation History

**Milan:** Home of **St. Ambrose** and later **Carlo Borromeo**, the Counter-Reformation archbishop who implemented the reforms of the **Council of Trent** most rigorously. Milan represents the Catholic response to Protestantism.

**Florence:** **Girolamo Savonarola (1452-1498)** was a Dominican friar who preached against corruption in the church and secular society decades before Luther. He briefly ruled Florence as a theocracy before being excommunicated and burned at the stake. Luther admired Savonarola as a proto-reformer.

### Key Sites (Florence)

- **San Marco Monastery** — Where Savonarola lived; his cell is preserved
- **Piazza della Signoria** — Where Savonarola was executed (plaque marks the spot)

### Practical Tips

- Italy uses **Euro**
- These are transit days with lots of driving — prioritize rest
- In Florence, if you have time, San Marco and the execution plaque are close together

---

## Day 10-11: Rome (3/25-26)

### Reformation History

Rome is where the **corruption Luther protested** was centered. The sale of **indulgences** to fund the building of **St. Peter's Basilica** was the direct trigger for the 95 Theses. Pope Leo X excommunicated Luther in 1521. Rome later hosted the **Council of Trent (1545-1563)**, which launched the Counter-Reformation. Luther himself visited Rome in 1510 as a young monk and was shocked by the corruption he witnessed — an experience that planted seeds of his later protest.

### Key Sites

- **St. Peter's Basilica** — The building whose construction financing sparked the Reformation
- **Vatican Museums / Sistine Chapel** — The wealth and artistic patronage that reformers criticized
- **Scala Santa (Holy Stairs)** — Luther reportedly climbed these on his knees in 1510 seeking merit, then stood up midway, realizing "the just shall live by faith"
- **Castel Sant'Angelo** — Papal fortress
- **San Paolo fuori le Mura** — Where Paul was traditionally martyred

### Practical Tips

- **Book Vatican tickets in advance** — lines can be 3+ hours without reservation
- Scala Santa is near St. John Lateran basilica, not at the Vatican
- Dress code for churches: covered shoulders and knees
- March weather: ~8-16°C, pleasant for walking
- Roma Pass can save money on transport and museums
